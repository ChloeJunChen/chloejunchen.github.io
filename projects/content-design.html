Content Design for a Tour Guide App

Understanding self-drive tourists’ unique information needs to design tailored content that enhances their travel experiences.

Background

Drive tourism is about freedom, exploration, and discovery, but traditional guided tours often fail to meet the needs of self-drive travelers who value flexibility and spontaneity. Audio guides, with their hands-free adaptability, are ideal for road trips but often lack content tailored to the journey’s context—whether it’s iconic sites, bustling cities, or expansive states. This study leverages schema theory to design intuitive, engaging audio guides that align with how travelers think, enhancing the road trip experience and making it more seamless and memorable.

Key Concepts

Before diving into the research, let’s explore some important terms and why they matter.

Drive Tourism

Unlike traditional guided tours with fixed schedules and group activities, drive tourism emphasizes freedom, flexibility, and self-directed exploration. Travelers navigate their journey by car, choosing where to stop, what to explore, and how long to stay. This travel style highlights the need for tools that provide tailored, adaptable information to enhance the experience.

Schema Theory

A cognitive framework that explains how people organize and interpret information based on prior knowledge and experiences. Applying schema theory to audio guide design ensures the content aligns with travelers' expectations, making it intuitive and easy to use.

Destination Levels

Different destination types—sites, cities, states, and countries—require tailored information. For example, site-level content might highlight key facts and photo tips, while country-level content provides logistics and cultural context. Addressing these levels ensures that content meets specific traveler needs at every stage of their journey.

Context-Aware Content Design

Context-awareness focuses on delivering relevant information based on a traveler’s location, preferences, and stage in their journey. This ensures that audio guides provide the right information at the right time, making the experience more engaging and less overwhelming.

Research Questions

This study explores how audio guide content can elevate the self-drive tourism experience, drawing on insights from literature and real-world observations. The research questions focus on bridging user needs with intuitive and engaging content design.

Information Needs Across Destinations

2. How do self-drive tourists’ information needs differ when navigating sites, cities, states, and countries?

Travelers engage with destinations at varying levels, each requiring different types of information. This question was proposed to ensure the audio guide addresses specific needs, such as factual details for sites, cultural highlights for cities, or safety information for countries, tailoring the experience to each context.

Schemas in Drive Tourism

1. What cognitive frameworks (schemas) shape the way self-drive tourists perceive and engage with their journeys?

Understanding the mental structures that drive tourism experiences is essential to creating audio guide content that resonates with travelers. This question was proposed to identify how themes like freedom, intimacy, or landscape appreciation influence travelers' expectations and behaviors, providing a foundation for designing intuitive and engaging content.

Improving Usability and Engagement

3. What design strategies can make audio guides both functionally effective and emotionally engaging for self-drive tourists?

Effective content design goes beyond providing information—it must also create an enjoyable and immersive experience. This question was proposed to explore how elements like storytelling, clarity, and schema alignment can enhance both usability and emotional connection, making the guide more impactful.

Methods

This user-centered study used interviews and qualitative analysis to uncover the needs, behaviors, and preferences of self-drive tourists, providing actionable insights for audio guide design.

Interview Guide

The interview guide was designed to uncover cognitive and emotional aspects of drive tourism, emphasizing user needs at different destination levels. Key sections included:

  • Travel Behaviors: Focused on understanding the motivations, spontaneity, and flexibility inherent to drive tourism. (“Can you describe your most recent road trip and the decisions you made along the way?”)
  • Information Needs: Probed how needs varied across destination levels, capturing practical and emotional requirements. (“What information did you find most helpful at different points of your trip (e.g., site, city, state)?”)
  • Audio Guide Preferences: Explored preferences for content delivery, style, and user experience. (“If you had an audio guide during this trip, what would you want it to provide, and how would you like it to deliver information?”)
  • Challenges and Pain Points: Highlighted gaps and opportunities for design improvements. (“What challenges or frustrations did you face in accessing or using information during your trip?”)

Data Collection

  • Participants: 27 U.S.-based participants with diverse demographics and travel experiences were recruited via Prolific Academic using theoretical sampling. Recruitment continued until theoretical saturation was reached.
  • Context: Participants reflected on recent road trips, from short getaways to multi-state journeys, providing fresh and varied perspectives.
  • Procedure: Semi-structured 40-minute interviews were conducted remotely via Zoom, enabling in-depth exploration and flexibility for participants to share their experiences.

Data Analysis

The collected data underwent a qualitative analysis to uncover patterns and actionable insights, with Miro used to organize and visualize data throughout the process.

  • Thematic Analysis: Followed Braun and Clarke’s six-step framework (familiarization, coding, theme identification, review, definition, and reporting) to identify recurring themes, including destination-specific needs, emotional engagement, and usability preferences.
  • Collaborative Validation: Two researchers independently coded the data, resolving discrepancies through discussions to refine themes.
  • UX Synthesis: Mapped themes to user needs and design strategies to inform practical audio guide features.

Key Results

The study identified core dimensions of drive tourism and revealed how information needs differ across destination levels, offering valuable insights for tailoring audio guide content.

Schemas of Drive Tourism

Identified five key dimensions defining the drive tourism experience:

  • Freedom: Emphasizes route flexibility, personal schedules, and item portability.
  • Intimacy: Highlights bonding experiences with family and companions.
  • Short Breaks: Provides mental rejuvenation and escape from routines.
  • In-depth ExplorationEncourages discovery of hidden gems and unexpected encounters.
  • Landscape Appreciation Enhances understanding of geographical and cultural contexts.

Design Impacts

The findings from this research provide actionable insights for designing engaging and tailored experiences for self-drive tourists. These impacts emphasize flexibility, personalization, and emotional resonance in content and service design.

Empowering Freedom Through Flexible Itineraries

Self-drive tourists prioritize autonomy and flexibility in their journeys.

Develop itinerary tools that allow users to customize routes, explore scenic detours, and discover off-the-beaten-path locations. Incorporate features like real-time updates and filters for preferences (e.g., scenic views, shortest route).

Strengthening Connections Through Shared Experiences

Bonding experiences, such as family activities, enhance emotional satisfaction during trips.

Develop family-focused features in travel apps, such as curated lists of family-friendly destinations, activity suggestions (e.g., group hikes or scenic picnic spots), and easy sharing options for plans with companions.

Enhancing Culinary Engagement Across Destinations

Culinary experiences connect users to the culture and identity of a destination.

Highlight local cuisine and dining options at the city level using vibrant imagery and curated recommendations. Showcase regional culinary traditions at the country level through immersive features like recipe integrations, food maps, or cultural context explanations.

Tailoring Content to Destination Contexts

Information needs vary by destination type, requiring adaptable and relevant content design.

At site level, provide concise facts, stories, and practical details (e.g., parking, amenities). At city level, emphasize local attractions, cultural events, and dining spots with visually rich content. At state level, focus on geographic overviews and key characteristics. At country level, offer logistical information like safety tips, cultural norms, and language support.

Facilitating Escapes with Relaxation-Focused Content

Travelers seeking mental rejuvenation value tranquil and nature-rich destinations.

Highlight peaceful locations in app content using calming visuals and clear categorizations like "Relaxation Spots" or "Quiet Getaways." Enable users to search for destinations based on relaxation-focused keywords.

Driving Exploration Through Hidden Gems

Discovering lesser-known attractions adds surprise and enhances the travel experience.

Integrate dynamic maps and recommendation engines that spotlight hidden gems, cultural landmarks, and local events. Use badges or icons to differentiate unique spots from mainstream attractions.

Making History Accessible and Engaging

History is universally appealing, with participants across all destination levels finding historical context both engaging and meaningful.

Embed historical narratives within content through interactive timelines, audio storytelling, or AR features. Ensure historical context is easily accessible at every destination level, from site-specific anecdotes to broader national histories, to deepen users' connections with the locations.
